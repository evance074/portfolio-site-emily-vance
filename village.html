Case Study: Designing a Digital Intervention for Postpartum Depression Support

Project Overview

This project aimed to design a digital intervention for raising awareness about postpartum depression (PPD) and its impact on maternal health. The focus was on creating an informative, supportive platform that would reach new mothers, healthcare providers, and caregivers with educational resources. Given the complexity of the health issue and the need for accurate information, this project was grounded in public health theory and research rather than traditional user interviews or testing.

This project was a unique challenge: to design a digital intervention aimed at increasing awareness of postpartum depression (PPD), reducing stigma, and encouraging early help-seeking. Unlike traditional UX projects, this work did not involve user interviews or usability testing due to the sensitive nature of the topic and early-stage conceptualization. Instead, the foundation was built entirely on public health theory, behavioral science, and in-depth desk research.

Rather than designing around user preferences, the process centered on understanding the clinical, social, and psychological factors surrounding PPD—and translating that into a meaningful, compassionate digital experience.

The Health Context

Postpartum depression (PPD) affects up to 20% of new mothers, with symptoms emerging within the first year of childbirth. The condition can last for months or even years, impacting both mental health and physical well-being. In some cases, the symptoms of PPD can remain undetected for long periods, making early intervention critical to improving outcomes. This case study explores the design of a solution to address these challenges, using the lens of public health theory and research.

Challenges and Insights

  1. Limited Detection and Delayed Treatment: Despite the significant impact of PPD, many women are not diagnosed or treated until much later. Often, the symptoms begin much earlier than women disclose to healthcare providers. This delay in detection can be linked to a lack of awareness or the stigma surrounding mental health issues.
  2. Stigma and Social Inequalities: Social factors such as poverty, discrimination, and a lack of social support can exacerbate the challenges of coping with PPD. Women who face multiple inequalities are at higher risk, and the stigma associated with mental health conditions can prevent them from seeking help. Addressing these social factors within the intervention design was critical for creating an effective solution.
  3. Complexity of the Health Issue: The relationship between PPD and factors like prenatal depression, stressful life events, and poor social support made it clear that a multifaceted approach was required. Additionally, as 30% of younger mothers (aged 25 and below) experience PPD, the design needed to be tailored to reach this specific demographic.

Project Requirements

To address these multifaceted challenges, the proposed digital experience included the following key components:

A Personalized Homepage

A calm, supportive landing space where mothers can track their emotional well-being over time, see uplifting affirmations or supportive tips, and access relevant tools or resources. Designed to feel like a check-in, not a diagnosis.

Community Connection Feature

Recognizing the power of peer support, this feature would allow mothers to connect anonymously with others experiencing similar emotions—via moderated forums, group chats, or shared stories—to reduce isolation and foster belonging.

Screening Tool for PPD

A self-guided, clinically validated screener (e.g. EPDS or PHQ-9) that allows users to check in with themselves and receive guidance on next steps. The tone would be warm and reassuring, reinforcing that seeking help is a sign of strength—not failure.

Resource Library

A curated library of local resources, articles, videos, and support materials about PPD, mental health, and self-care practices, with content tailored to different stages of the postpartum journey. This would help users feel informed, empowered, and less alone.

Partner Companion App

An optional, pared-down version of the app for partners, designed to raise awareness and offer tools to check in on the mother’s emotional state and learn how to provide better support. This supports shared responsibility and encourages proactive caregiving.

Ask for Help Button

A frictionless way for mothers to request help from pre-selected contacts or local services—whether that means a ride to an appointment, a babysitting break, or emotional support. This would be customizable and designed to minimize guilt or shame.

Design Process and Evolution

Although this project didn't involve interviews or user testing, I moved through a full design cycle—from low-fidelity sketches to high-fidelity prototypes—using public health research and feedback from peers in related fields to guide decisions.

Low-Fidelity Sketches

To start, I sketched core user flows by hand to explore structure and content needs. These early sketches focused on:

  • Homepage: A personalized landing space for mothers to track their emotional well-being.
  • Community Connection: A feature to connect mothers anonymously with others experiencing similar emotions.
  • Screening Tool: A self-guided, clinically validated screener for PPD.
  • Resource Library: A curated library of local resources and articles about PPD.
  • Partner Companion App: A pared-down version of the app for partners.

High-Fidelity Prototypes

At the high-fidelity stage, I focused on elevating usability and emotional tone through visual design. Key changes and additions included:

  • A revised color scheme that introduced soft, calming greens to reflect emotional support and reduce clinical coldness.
  • A new bottom navigation layout, placing the "You" dashboard first to reinforce user-centered care.
  • A redesigned login page that included a distinct login option for partners, signaling their unique role in the caregiving ecosystem.

Iteration Based on Public Health Peer Feedback

I presented the prototype to a small group of public health graduate student peers to gather domain-specific feedback. Based on their insights, I made several critical updates:

Redesigned the Dashboard ("You") to include a visual timeline and an updated check-in button for faster access

Added a Results Page after the PPD quiz, providing immediate feedback, supportive language, and recommended resources.

Renamed the “Feed” to Community, and added navigation tabs for Community Posts and Community Events to encourage both online and offline connection.

Design System

The prototype was built using Material Design 3, chosen for its accessibility, robust component system, and familiarity in health-tech interfaces. MD3 helped ensure consistency across states, interactions, and screen sizes, while still allowing flexibility for visual softness and personalization.

Reflection

This project pushed me to think differently about what UX can look like—especially when the “users” are not yet at the table. Without direct interviews or usability testing, I leaned heavily on public health theory, behavioral research, and clinical data to inform design decisions. That experience deepened my respect for the nuances of health-related design work, especially when dealing with topics like stigma, mental health, and social inequality.

Designing for postpartum depression required a trauma-informed lens, an equity-centered mindset, and constant reflection on how design choices could either reinforce or reduce stigma. Every feature—whether a screening quiz or a community chat—had to balance clinical accuracy with emotional safety.

Through this process, I also became more confident in advocating for multi-user experiences (like the partner app), and I saw the importance of designing not just for individuals but for their ecosystems of care. Receiving feedback from public health peers reminded me that cross-disciplinary collaboration strengthens design outcomes, especially in complex issue areas.

If this project were to move forward, I'd prioritize co-design sessions with new mothers, healthcare providers, and community support networks to validate assumptions and ensure the tool is relevant, accessible, and emotionally safe.
